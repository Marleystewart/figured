01 · Executive Summary

What 4ward is, in one page.

Mission

Help every college student see where their path is actually leading, and what it'll honestly take to get where they want to go.

Vision

Every student at every U.S. college has a 4ward profile by senior year, the same way they have a LinkedIn. Becomes the honest layer between school and career.

Core value proposition

Career tools today are either scoreboards (judgmental) or job boards (transactional). 4ward is the first one that talks to a student like a real mentor: honest, specific to them, never less ambitious than they want to be, always ending on a move they can make today.

Competitive edge

  • Voice as a moat. The Honest Take + Ask 4ward chat is tuned to mentor language, not chatbot language. Nobody else has invested in this.
  • Goal-aware from day one. Sports, medicine, arts, finance: works for non-traditional goals other tools fumble.
  • Built by the customer. Two college students. We are the user. That's a credibility advantage Handshake and LinkedIn cannot buy.
  • Cheap to run. Static site on Vercel free tier. AI cost is pay-as-you-use. No burn.

Target market summary

U.S. four-year college students aged 18 to 23. ~16 million of them. First wedge: students with non-traditional or "ambitious but scary" goals at four to six warm-network schools (Duke, Michigan, your own networks). Why this wedge: they're underserved by every existing tool and they tell their friends when something works.

Monetization summary

Free forever for students (this is the brand promise). Revenue comes from schools and career centers who license 4ward for their student body, and employers/mentors who pay for verified profile access. Year one: collect waitlist + signed letters of interest from career centers. Year two: first paid school pilots.

Long-term scalability

It's a static web app with a tiny backend (eventually). Every new student is essentially free to serve. Mentor side is opt-in and self-curating. Real moat is the dataset: as more students log wins and update profiles, 4ward builds the only honest map of how students actually become professionals.

02 · 12-Month Roadmap

Month by month, what we're actually doing.

Each month has one objective. We do that one thing well, then move on. The biggest startup-killer for student founders is overcommitting on month 1.

Month 1 · Validate with 25 real students

$0

Goal: confirm 4ward solves a real problem before adding anything new.

  • DM 25 college students across 5 schools. Watch them use the live app.
  • Ask: "If we charged your school for this, would you tell them to pay?" Track yes/no.
  • Log every confused moment in a Notion. No fixes yet, just listen.
  • Write 1 honest reflection at the end of week 4 on what we heard.

Risk: we get excited and skip this. Success: 25 sessions logged, 5 patterns surfaced. Tools: Notion (free), iMessage, Zoom (free).

Month 2 · Fix the top 3 things, get an LLC

~$150

Goal: act on the validation, formalize the business.

  • Patch the top 3 confusion points from Month 1.
  • File a Delaware or home-state LLC (~$100-$200 depending on state). Pick a state in the legal section below.
  • Get an EIN (free, IRS website, 10 minutes).
  • Open a free business checking account (Mercury or Relay, no minimums).

Risk: spending too much on legal. Success: LLC filed, EIN in hand. Tools: Stripe Atlas ($500 if you want it done for you, optional) or LegalZoom.

Month 3 · Lock the brand, ship resume-upload v2

~$30

Goal: look like a real company, ship a feature that wows.

  • Buy figured.com (or figured.app, figured.co — check section 4). ~$15-50/year.
  • Lock the visual identity: typography, color, logo lockups, a 1-page brand sheet in Figma (free).
  • Ship resume-upload improvements: support .docx, better feedback rubric, save resume server-side (intro the backend, see Month 5).

Risk: design rabbit-hole. Success: domain live, brand sheet exists, resume v2 in prod. Tools: Namecheap, Figma, Vercel.

Month 4 · Beta launch at 2 schools

~$25

Goal: get to 100 weekly active students.

  • Pick 2 schools where you have real friends (yours + Alex's).
  • Run small-stakes campus campaigns: GroupMe, dorm chats, Greek life, athletic teams.
  • Make 3 TikToks per week from each of you. Voice: honest, useful, no corporate.
  • Stand up a waitlist for "4ward at YOUR school."

Risk: launching too wide. Success: 100 WAU, 250 waitlist signups. Tools: TikTok, Instagram, GroupMe, Plausible analytics (free tier).

Month 5 · Real accounts, real backend

~$20

Goal: students can save their progress across devices.

  • Add accounts via Clerk or Supabase Auth (both have free tiers under 10k users).
  • Move localStorage data server-side. Mentor list, profile, wins, all in Supabase (free tier).
  • Stop asking students for an API key. AI costs move onto our side (still tiny at this scale).

Risk: Claude API costs creep up. Success: backend live, every login persists. Tools: Supabase (free), Clerk (free), Vercel (free).

Month 6 · First career-center conversation

~$0

Goal: one signed letter of interest from one career center.

  • Cold email 20 career-center directors at mid-size schools. Lead with student outcomes, not features.
  • Book 5 zoom demos.
  • Ask them: "What would you need to see for your school to pay for this?"
  • Write down the answer. That's your sales script.

Risk: burning the warm contacts. Success: 1 LOI in hand. Tools: Gmail, Cal.com (free), Loom.

Month 7 · Mentor system at 50 mentors

~$20

Goal: turn mentor opt-in into a real two-sided network.

  • Recruit 50 mentors via LinkedIn + alumni networks. Skew toward non-traditional paths (sports, arts, startups).
  • Send each mentor a clean "How 4ward Works" loom + your direct ask.
  • Add a mentor dashboard so they can see how many students viewed their card.

Risk: mentors sign up but never engage. Success: 50 active mentors, 30%+ have been clicked. Tools: LinkedIn, Loom, Mailchimp (free under 500 contacts).

Month 8 · First revenue. Any revenue.

~$25

Goal: $500 of paid revenue in the door. Doesn't matter from where.

  • Convert the Month 6 LOI into a paid pilot, even at $250.
  • Or: launch a "4ward for Athletes" or "4ward for Pre-Med" paid version at $9/mo. Don't be precious about charging.
  • Or: career-coach affiliate revenue: 20% rev share on referrals.

Risk: we feel weird charging students. We're not charging students. We're charging schools and adults. Success: $500 collected. Tools: Stripe.

Month 9 · Scale systems, not effort

~$40

Goal: serve 1,000 students without working more.

  • Set up real analytics dashboards (Plausible or PostHog free tier).
  • Move customer support to a simple intake form + Loom replies. No live chat yet.
  • Write 5 SOPs: onboarding a school, recruiting a mentor, handling a bug report, writing a TikTok, weekly review.

Risk: we become a help desk. Success: 1,000 students, <5 hours/week of operational drag. Tools: Notion SOPs, PostHog, Tally.

Month 10 · First hire (probably an intern)

$0-$500/mo

Goal: a 3rd person doing 1 well-defined job.

  • Hire a campus ambassador or paid intern from your own school. Not co-founder, not equity.
  • Their job: run TikTok and recruit campus reps at 3 new schools.
  • Pay them in cash. Skip equity at this stage.

Risk: we hire too early and burn cash. Success: first hire is producing 5x their salary in growth. Tools: Handshake, your own school, Gusto if/when needed.

Month 11 · Investor readiness (light)

$0

Goal: be fundable if you want to be.

  • Build the data room: financials, user metrics, retention, traction, screenshots, founder story.
  • Write a 10-slide deck. (Y Combinator's "How to write a great pitch deck" is the only source you need.)
  • Apply to YC, Z Fellows, Pear, Soma Capital, On Deck Fellows. All free to apply.

Risk: distraction from product. Success: applications submitted, deck exists. Tools: Pitch.com (free), Notion data room.

Month 12 · Expansion + 12-month review

$50

Goal: write the second 12-month plan from a real base.

  • 3-5 schools live, 2,500 student accounts, $2-5k MRR or one signed school contract.
  • Take 1 week off. Then write the next plan.
  • Reflect: what did we wrongly assume in Month 1?

Risk: burnout. Success: v2 plan signed by both founders. Tools: a long walk.

04 · Legal & IP

The smallest amount of legal you actually need.

LLC vs C-Corp (right now: LLC)

Form an LLC in Month 2. It's cheap, simple, and right for two co-founders. The C-Corp conversion is something you do only if you're raising venture money (Month 11+). Most YC startups are Delaware C-Corps but you'll convert when the time comes. Don't pre-pay for what you don't need.

State recommendation

  • Easy mode: form in your home state (~$100). Simplest taxes.
  • Investor mode (only if you're sure you'll raise): Stripe Atlas, Delaware C-Corp, $500. Includes EIN + bank account.

The checklist in order

  1. Month 2: File LLC (state website or LegalZoom, ~$100-$200).
  2. Month 2: Get EIN from IRS.gov (free, 10 min).
  3. Month 2: Open Mercury or Relay bank account (free).
  4. Month 2: Sign a co-founder agreement. Even a 1-page Google Doc. Cover: equity split, vesting (recommend 4-year vest, 1-year cliff), what happens if one of you leaves.
  5. Month 3: Draft Terms of Service + Privacy Policy. Use Termly or Iubenda (~$10/mo) or copy + adapt a simple SaaS template.
  6. Month 4: File trademark for "4ward" (USPTO TEAS Plus, $250). Do a free TESS search first.
  7. Month 8: When you hit revenue, hire a real accountant for $500-$1,000 to file taxes. Don't DIY this.

IP guidance

  • Trademark: "4ward" as a word mark + the leaf logo. File once you have real users (Month 4).
  • Patents: Don't bother. Software patents are expensive and the product isn't patentable.
  • Copyright: Code is auto-copyrighted. The repo being public doesn't waive copyright.
  • NDAs: Skip them in 99% of conversations. Investors won't sign them and shouldn't.

Liability + compliance

  • Privacy policy must mention what data you store (currently localStorage only).
  • Once you add accounts (Month 5): you become a data controller. FERPA implications if you partner with schools. Read it once before signing your first school contract.
  • Don't store API keys server-side until you have to. Each user's key in their browser = no liability for breach.

05 · Branding

The voice is the brand. Don't dilute it.

Positioning statement

For college students who are tired of being told they're "on the right track" or "behind," 4ward is a mentor in your pocket that tells you the truth about where your path is actually leading and exactly what'll move you closer. Unlike Handshake (a job board) or LinkedIn (a resume), 4ward is the only career tool built to feel like a real conversation with someone who gets you.

Tone of voice

  • Honest without being brutal. "Here's what it takes" not "Here's what you're missing."
  • Specific. Every sentence should be unusable for any other student.
  • Forward. Never ends on a verdict. Always ends on a move, a question, or one sentence of belief.
  • No em dashes. They read as AI.

Visual identity

  • Primary color: deep forest green (#2f5a4f). Calming, grounded, "honest."
  • Canvas: warm off-white (#f6f4ee). Paper, not screen.
  • Type: Inter, 800 weight headlines. Confident, modern, free.
  • Logo: a leaf with a curl. Growth, not climb.

Tagline candidates

  • "See where your path is actually leading." (current homepage)
  • "A mentor, in your pocket."
  • "The honest layer between school and career."
  • "4ward. Because no one tells you the truth about your path."

Content pillars

  1. Honest takes on student career advice (what's actually true vs what counselors say).
  2. Path stories: how non-traditional careers actually happen.
  3. "Mentor minute" short videos: 30-second answers to real student questions.
  4. Behind-the-build: two college founders shipping in public.

Paid (only after Month 8)

Don't run paid ads until you have real conversion data. When you do:

  • Start with $50 TikTok promote on a high-performing organic video. Not "ads."
  • Test campus subreddits at $1 CPC ceilings.

The 5-school strategy

Don't try to launch at every school. Pick 5 where you know people. Saturate. Show traction. Then expand. Suggested order:

  1. Your school + Alex's school (Month 4)
  2. 2 friends-of-friends schools (Month 6)
  3. 1 strategic "name brand" school for credibility (Month 9)

Referral system

Built into the product: "Share your trajectory" link. When a student shares it with 3 friends and they sign up, they unlock free premium for a year. Costs nothing, drives loops.

Partnerships to pursue

  • Career centers: the long game. Start in Month 6.
  • Student athlete advisors: NIL programs are flush. Pitch 4ward as "honest career planning for athletes."
  • First-gen student orgs: the audience that needs this most.
  • Athletic conferences (Ivy, ACC, SEC): after Month 9. They have student-development budgets.

PR strategy

Pitch student newspapers first. Easy wins, good for the "in 5 schools" story. Then pitch Forbes 30 Under 30, Inc, TechCrunch Disrupt at Month 9 once metrics are real.

08 · Launch Strategy

You already launched. Now launch again, better.

4ward is live but unpolished. The "real launch" is Month 5 when accounts ship. Treat that as the milestone.

Pre-launch checklist (by end of Month 4)

  • Resume upload works smoothly for both PDF and .docx
  • AI loading states have personality (the "mentor thinking" feel)
  • Landing page sharpened, no placeholder copy
  • Accounts work, sync across devices
  • Mobile is genuinely good (not "okay")
  • Privacy policy + ToS published
  • 100-person waitlist warmed up

Launch week (Month 5)

WeekendPersonal outreach: text 50 friends each. Real ones. Not a blast.

If launch struggles

Don't panic-add features. The 3 most common launch problems and the fix:

  • Low signups: wrong message, not wrong product. Rewrite the hero. Iterate weekly.
  • Signups but no engagement: onboarding friction. Cut a step. Measure.
  • Engagement but no shares: share UX is bad. Add one-tap share link.

10 · Sales

School career centers are the primary sale.

Funnel

  1. Top: cold email or warm intro to a career-center director
  2. Middle: 20-minute demo, ask discovery questions
  3. Bottom: pilot proposal (free first semester, $X/student after)
  4. Close: signed pilot agreement (one-page LOI is enough at first)

Pricing psychology

  • Never lead with price. Lead with student outcomes.
  • Price per student, not per seat. They think in students.
  • First school is half-price or free. You need the case study.

Sample cold email

Subject: Built by students, for your students

Hi [Director],

I'm Marley, a sophomore at [school]. My co-founder Alex and I built a tool called 4ward because every career app we tried felt either judgmental or transactional. It's an honest mentor-style trajectory tool for students. It's already used by ~250 students across 3 schools.

Could I send a 90-second loom of what it does and what a pilot at [their school] could look like? Free for the first semester.

Thanks,
Marley

Retention

The product retains itself through the over-time loop. But for paying schools: monthly impact email with their school's aggregate (anonymized) metrics. "Your students logged 1,200 wins this month."

11 · Scaling

From 1 school to 100. From 2 founders to 5.

Bootstrap vs raise

Bootstrap through Month 12. If your real growth rate is >10% month-over-month at month 9, raising a small round (~$250k pre-seed) in Month 12 makes sense to hire a designer + a backend engineer.

When to raise

  • Don't raise until you have either: 1 signed paying school, or 1,500 weekly active students.
  • Raise small first. $250k pre-seed from angels/Z Fellows beats a $1M seed from a fund that wants 10% and a board seat.
  • Target valuation if you raise: $5-$8M post (standard pre-seed for SaaS with traction).

14 · Founder Action Plan

If you only do these things, you'll win.

Top 10 highest-ROI actions

  1. Talk to 25 students in Month 1. No exceptions.
  2. Ship the AI loading states + landing polish before launching wider.
  3. File the LLC in Month 2. Get the boring stuff done early.
  4. Post on TikTok 3x/week each, every week. The compounding is the whole strategy.
  5. Email 20 career-center directors in Month 6. Even bad responses are signal.
  6. Recruit 50 mentors who can't say no because you know them.
  7. Charge somebody, anybody, by Month 8.
  8. Write SOPs in Month 9 so you can hire in Month 10.
  9. Apply to Y Combinator + Z Fellows in Month 11. Free shot.
  10. Take 1 week off in Month 12. Then plan v2.

The biggest mistakes to avoid

  • Adding features instead of finding users.
  • Spending money to "look real." Real is real.
  • Raising too early.
  • Pretending to be users you're not (a freshman pretending to be a senior in your demo will be obvious).
  • Forgetting that your competition is "the student doing nothing." Most students aren't on Handshake either.

Daily founder habits

  • 15 minutes of customer talk (DMs, emails, replies).
  • 1 ship per day (a fix, a post, a cold email, anything).
  • 1 honest sentence at end of day: what worked, what didn't.

Weekly review (Sunday, 30 min)

  • What's our WAU this week vs last?
  • What did one user tell us we didn't know?
  • What are the 3 things we're doing next week?

Monthly review (last day of month, 1 hour)

  • Did we hit the month's one objective?
  • Are the KPIs trending right?
  • What do we cut next month?

What separates startups that win

The startups that survive Year 1 share three things: they shipped weekly, they talked to users weekly, and they didn't quit when month 4 felt flat. None of those require money. All of them require showing up.

You already shipped 4ward. That's the hardest part. Now the work is showing up every week for a year.
